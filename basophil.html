Lifestyle & Behavior Strategies

1. Dietary

a. Drink Plenty of Fluids! Normal is considered 64-70 oz of total fluid per day.

b. Maintain a healthy weight. Increased body weight has been associated with increased risk of both stress and urge UI.

  • This decreases bladder irritability
  • Helps to prevent urinary tract infections
  • Helps to avoid constipation

2. Limit or Decrease Bladder Irritants

  • Spicy foods such as citrus juices,
    or tomato-based foods

3. Increase high-fiber foods

Core Strengthening

Provides strength and stability to enhance pelvic floor function

Examples of core strengthening exercises include leg lifts, bird dog, and plank which have been proven to generate pelvic floor muscle squeeze pressures equal to or greater than concentrated pelvic floor muscle contractions

Proper Toileting Techniques

When using the restroom, sit up straight and if possible, elevate your knees to be angled at a higher degree than your hips. You can utilize a step stool or even just elevate your heels to help elevate your knees. This allows for more complete bladder emptying. Remember, don’t push when using the restroom!

Double Voiding

Double voiding is simply waiting for a second bladder contraction after your initial urine stream. Normally, if we sit long enough, for example an extra 20-30 seconds, our bladder will contract again, allowing our bladder to empty more completely.

Urge Suppression Techniques: (Quick Flick Kegel Contractions)

Urge suppression techniques (or Quick Flicks) are used to help prevent urinary urgency, frequency and leakage associated with these symptoms. When you feel an urge, your first instinct is to rush to the bathroom. However, what you want to do is to stand still and perform five 2-second contractions (loop to Quick flick animation).

Afterwards, continue to stand still and perform distraction techniques, such as counting backwards from 10. Contracting your pelvic floor sends a reflex back to the bladder, allowing it to relax and alleviate a bladder contraction and give you more time to get to the bathroom. Goals of urge suppression techniques include teaching control of strong bladder urges, decreasing frequent voiding and increasing bladder capacity.

Myth

Incontinence is an inevitable part of being a woman or aging. It’s not! There are many strategies to reduce or eliminate urinary incontinence.

A strong pelvic floor improves quality of life: keeps organs in their anatomical place, contracts reflexively against intraabdominal pressure (laughing, coughing, sneezing, bending). The muscles in your pelvic floor have a tone than the other muscles in your body.

Types of Urinary Incontinence

Stress Urinary Incontinence (SUI)

Stress UI occurs when pelvic floor muscles no longer support the pelvic organs, most often the bladder. If pelvic floor muscles are weak, abrupt changes in intra-abdominal pressure are not equally transmitted to the bladder and urethra.

Symptoms of SUI

Symptoms of SUI: leakage occurring with laughing, coughing, sneezing, heavy lifting, exercise activities, bending, etc.

Urge Urinary Incontinence (UUI)

Urge UI is involuntary bladder contractions that create a sense of urinary urgency and frequency and is defined as urinary frequency more than 7x/day or getting up at night 2 or more times. Urge UI does not have to be associated with urinary leakage.

Symptoms of UUI

Urinary urgency associated with running water, seeing a toilet, cold weather, and when coming home from being out.

Mixed Urinary Incontinence (MUI)

This is a combination of both stress UI and Urge UI. Women experience both leakage associated with stress maneuvers as well as urinary urgency/frequency with or without urinary leakage.

Functional Urinary Incontinence (FUI)

Occurs when the bladder is working normally, however, there is an inability to get to the bathroom in time, whether due to physical, cognitive or environmental conditions.

Symptoms of FUI

Some of the causes of functional incontinence include problems with walking (arthritis or cerebral palsy) and problems with memory or learning (such as dementia and intellectual disability).

Pelvic Muscle Exercises

Type I - (Slow-twitch muscles fibers) Characterized by low force/power/speed production and high endurance

Practice KNACK: Five slower Kegel contractions when you feel the urge to go, then wait 10 seconds before heading to the bathroom. The contractions send a message to your bladder to relax, helping to calm the bladder contractions. Keep practicing this to decrease the urgency to run to the bathroom.

Type II – (Fast-twitch muscle fibers) Characterized by high force/power/speed production and low endurance

Stress incontinence occurs because the urethral sphincter cannot withstand high pressures when coughing, sneezing, laughing, bending or exercising, causing leakage of urine. The fast twitch muscle fibers contract reflexively against increases in intraabdominal pressured during laughing, coughing, sneezing and bending.

Use “Quick Flicks” exercise to prevent leaks: practice Kegel contractions before, during and after triggering events, such as a cough. This will strengthen the muscles overtime around the bladder and urethra to prevent leaks.

Behavioral Strategies

Pelvic Floor Muscle Exercises

Use it or lose it!

1. Comprised of Skeletal muscle – responds to exercise like any other muscle.

2. Endurance contractions incorporate the Type I muscle fibers (slow-twitch) and are referred to as “marathon runners”. The levator ani (puborectalis, iliococcygeus and pubococcygeus) comprise mostly slow-twitch muscle fibers and are responsible for sustained pelvic floor contractions. They comprise approximately 65% of the pelvic floor and are characterized by low force/power/speed, but sustained pelvic.

3. Contraction of the pelvic floor muscles compresses around the urethra, increasing resistance to prevent leakage.

4. Stengthening these muscles increases muscle mass, allowing for a more effective contraction, whether to prevent stress or urge UI.

5. Provides proprioception to enhance sexuality.

Urge Suppression Techniques

1. When you feel the urge, don’t rush. Instead stand still and perform 5 quick pelvic floor contractions (referred to as Quick Flicks). Contraction of the pelvic floor causes a reflex back to the bladder and calms the bladder. Perform distraction techniques while standing or sitting (can count backwards from 10) and then slowly walk to the bathroom as the urge subsides.

2. Quick Flicks incorporate mostly the Type II muscle fibers (fast-twitch) referred to as “sprinters”. The ischiocavernosus, bulbospongiosus comprise mostly fast-twitch muscle fibers and are responsible for quick, rapid, short contractions.
